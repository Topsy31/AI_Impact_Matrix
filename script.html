The Invisible Trade — Presenter Script

20 minutes · 5 movements
Movement 1 of 5
The Comfortable Story
3 minutes  ·  Matrix blank, no spheres
Open with the matrix blank. No spheres visible. Do not explain what the matrix is yet. "Picture someone you know — probably yourself — sitting with a difficult document to write. An important one. A proposal, a strategy paper, a performance review. You have been staring at it for twenty minutes and it isn't moving. So you open ChatGPT, describe the situation, and it gives you a first draft in thirty seconds. You edit it, improve it, send it. The work is done. The output is good. Everyone is happy." Pause. Let it sit. At least three seconds. "That story feels completely fine. That's the problem."
Plant the central question — you will return to this at the very end "What are you paying for what AI gives you?" Do not answer it. Move on immediately.
Movement 2 of 5
Building the Frame
7 minutes  ·  Build the matrix live using the left blade
→ Click Value axis in the left blade "The first question the matrix asks is not 'did AI help you?' It asks: did you retain anything? Not the output — the output is obvious. Did the human who used the tool come away more capable, or less?" Pause. Let that distinction settle.
→ Click Engagement axis "The second question is about what kind of human activity was involved. Intellectual engagement — reasoning, evaluating, constructing an argument — or emotional engagement — connection, identity, the sense of being understood. These are not equivalent. AI interacts with them differently."
→ Click Neurology axis "The third question is whose brain we're talking about. A brain that is fully formed, or one that is still forming. And — this is the part the mainstream AI debate almost entirely ignores — a neurotypical brain, or a neurodivergent one." "For people with ADHD, autism, or dyslexia, AI can be a genuine equaliser. Not a workaround — a structural equaliser. The real-time task structuring, the communication scaffolding, the removal of friction between how someone actually thinks and how the neurotypically-designed workplace expects them to present. Neurodiverse workers in recent studies report significantly higher satisfaction with AI tools than neurotypical respondents. That matters, and it belongs in this picture." "But the same axis cuts the other way. One study found that the phrase 'I have autism' scored more negatively in AI language model associations than 'I am a bank robber.' The tool that is helping someone may simultaneously be encoding stigma about them. That tension is real, unresolved, and worth naming." Deliver the neurodivergent aside directly, not apologetically. State the benefit before the risk.
→ Click Adult spheres, then Child spheres "We now have eight zones. Eight different answers to the question of what AI is doing to human capability. Let's find where you are."
Movement 3 of 5  ·  Part A
Sphere 1 — The Aspirational Cell
Intellectual · Value Added · Neurotypical (Adult)
Click the blue sphere at Intellectual · Added · Typical. The right blade opens. Read the headline to the room. "This is the aspirational cell. This is where you believe you are when you open ChatGPT. Hint-seeking. Augmenting your thinking, not replacing it. Twenty-seven percent of users in one study stayed here. They showed no meaningful cognitive degradation. The question is — are you one of them?" "And how would you know?" Plant the seed. Do not answer it. Move on immediately.
Movement 3 of 5  ·  Parts B & C
The Invisible Trap & The Irreversible Harm
8 minutes total across both spheres
Sphere 2 — Intellectual · Value Reduced · Neurotypical (Adult). Click the purple sphere. "This is the most dangerous cell in the matrix. Not because the harm is severe — but because it is invisible. The professional in this cell produces output that still looks good. Their colleagues cannot see the problem. Their manager cannot see it. They cannot see it. The skill is eroding quietly, below the level of awareness, and the only moment the loss becomes visible is when the AI is taken away."
Tell Gloria Mark's finding as a story — not a citation. No need to say "a study found." "Gloria Mark has been studying human attention since 2003. In her lab studies, the average time a person could hold focus on a single task was two and a half minutes. By 2020 it was forty-seven seconds. She said cognitive abilities atrophy through disuse — exactly like a muscle. And people with weakened critical thinking become more susceptible to misinformation." "She was talking about smartphones. Imagine the same mechanism, but faster, more targeted, and designed to feel helpful."
Sphere 3 — Intellectual · Value Reduced · Neurotypical (Child). Click the red sphere. "Now I want you to consider a child. Not a professional who is losing a skill they once had. A child who is never forming it in the first place." "A cohort study tracked five hundred children from infancy. It found that the children with the highest screen exposure at age one — not age ten, age one — showed the largest academic effects years later. The mechanism was not the screens themselves. It was what the screens displaced. Every hour in front of a screen was an hour not spent in effortful, reciprocal interaction with a caregiver. That interaction is how cognitive architecture gets built. Remove it, and the architecture doesn't form."
The sharpest line in the presentation. Slow down. Deliver it clearly. "An adult who stops practising loses a skill they once had. A child who never practises never builds it. Those are not the same problem. Non-formation may be permanent in ways that adult atrophy is not." Let the silence hold. Do not fill it. At least five seconds.
Movement 4 & 5 of 5
The Evidence & The Return
2 minutes  ·  No new data. No new slides.
Switch to Research Graph view. Let the audience absorb it before speaking. Ten seconds minimum. "Everything I've described is grounded in peer-reviewed research. This is the network of papers underpinning the matrix — each sphere connected to the studies that support it, and those studies connected to each other through citation. The papers at the centre of that network are the ones that bear the most evidential weight."
Return to the matrix view. Return to the opening story. Close with the question you planted at the start. "Back to that document. The proposal, the strategy paper, the performance review. The output was good. Everyone was happy. Here is the question I want to leave you with — and I'm not going to answer it for you, because I don't think there's a single answer:" "What did you pay for that thirty seconds?" "Not in money. In capability. In the neural pathways that would have fired if you had struggled with it for another twenty minutes. In the self-knowledge you would have gained about how you actually think about that problem. In the thing you would have noticed, halfway through writing it yourself, that you couldn't have predicted you'd notice." "I'm not arguing you should stop using AI. I use it every day. I wrote a book teaching people how. What I'm arguing is that we are making this trade constantly, automatically, without awareness — and we are making it for our children before they can consent to it." "The matrix doesn't tell you what to do. It just shows you the territory. Tonight I'd like to hear where you think you are in it — and whether you've ever asked yourself that question before." End on the question. The conversation that follows is the point.
